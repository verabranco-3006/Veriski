# Vision

Scale engineering operations through intelligent automation and AI-powered process execution—building trusted frameworks that reduce friction, minimize risk, and enable Engineering to move fast with operational confidence.

# Values

| **Operational Excellence** | **Continuous Improvement & Innovation** | **AI-First Automation** | **Trusted Partnership** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Achieve Operational Discipline by codifying best practices and full adherence to eliminate systemic risks and drive quality. | Champion a Kaizen culture by leveraging metrics and AI to identify systemic friction, drive data-backed improvements, pioneer innovative approaches, and build organizational capability through continuous learning and experimentation. | Embrace AI as the primary driver of automation—leveraging intelligent technologies and advanced platforms to replace manual work, accelerate delivery, reduce operational risk, and scale process execution with minimal human intervention. | Enable Shared Success through transparency, integrity, and clear accountability, moving from a "policing" role to a true Engineering enabler. |

# Obstacles

| **Obstacle** | **Summary** | **Impact** |
|---|---|---|
| **Regulatory Deadlines & Capacity Constraints** | FedRAMP deadlines create non-negotiable capacity surges during Q2 2026, consuming substantial design and documentation effort. Simultaneously, transformation work (M1-M5) requires sustained 70% capacity allocation while operational tasks (M6) have unpredictable surges from audit season, CritSits, and urgent support. This dual pressure risks either operational degradation or transformation stagnation. | <ul><li>M1 (FedRAMP): Risk of delays in 3PAO readiness</li><li>M6 (Sustainability): Q2 operational capacity spikes to 40%+ vs 30% target</li><li>M1-M5: Transformation milestones delayed due to operational firefighting</li><li>Team burnout from sustained high-intensity work</li><li>Quality degradation in either operations or transformation</li></ul> |
| **AI Skill Gap & Platform Maturity** | Transitioning to "AI-First" and "Process as Code" (Method 5) requires the team to rapidly master AI @ Product platform, AI tool usage, and automation design while maintaining current operations—creating a steep learning curve with no formal training program. Compounding this challenge, the AI @ Product platform and Process as Code capabilities are emerging/immature, with platform limitations, lack of documentation, or technical issues potentially delaying industrialization work. | <ul><li>M5.1 (AI Enablement): Risk of slow adoption or superficial AI usage</li><li>M5.3 (Process Industrialization): Pilot skill deployment may miss Q1 2027 target</li><li>M5.3d (Technical Capability): Team certification blocked by platform immaturity</li><li>M5.3c (Backlog Readiness): Blueprints may not translate if platform constraints unknown</li><li>M2 (Change): Automation discovery relies on AI capability</li><li>Team burnout from learning + operating simultaneously</li><li>Wasted effort if platform architecture changes mid-flight</li></ul> |
| **Data Quality & Trust in Automated Governance** | Shifting from human reviews to AI-driven, system-enforced logic (Methods 2, 3, 5) requires absolute stakeholder confidence in technical data and automation. Fragmented data entry across Jira projects and lack of automated links between logs and version control risk diluting CPTO Metric accuracy (Method 4). If stakeholders don't trust metrics or automation produces incorrect results, data-driven decision-making fails and teams regress to manual approvals—negating velocity and scalability gains. | <ul><li>M4 (Metrics): Risk of low adoption or stakeholder skepticism</li><li>M2 (Change): Reviewer empowerment and CFR correlation may face resistance or accuracy issues</li><li>M3 (Incident): Triage automation and alert validation may be second-guessed</li><li>M5.3 (Process Industrialization): Engineering teams may not adopt PE Skills</li><li>M6 (Capacity): Story point tracking unreliable if data inconsistent</li><li>Executive decisions based on flawed data = credibility crisis</li><li>Credibility risk if automation produces incorrect results</li></ul> |
| **Cross-Organizational Dependencies & Alignment** | Success depends on collaboration with teams beyond Process Engineering (GS, SRE, Engineering teams, AI @ Product platform team). Misalignment, competing priorities, or delays from dependencies can block progress. Workshop findings already documented GS/Engineering alignment challenges, confirming this as a known risk area. | <ul><li>M1 (FedRAMP): Requires GS and SRE collaboration for operational model</li><li>M3 (Incident): Retrospective transformation needs SRE and Value Stream Leader buy-in</li><li>M5.3 (Process Industrialization): Depends on AI @ Product platform maturity and team availability</li><li>M2 (Change): Reviewer empowerment needs CAB and Engineering alignment</li><li>Delays from external team dependencies can cascade across multiple methods</li></ul> |
| **Career Framework Adoption & Talent Retention** | Introducing a career framework (M5.2) raises expectations for advancement and compensation. If advancement is blocked by budget/headcount constraints or the framework doesn't resonate with the team, retention risk increases instead of decreases. Talent attrition disrupts AI enablement, automation work, and organizational capability building—undermining the entire transformation. | <ul><li>M5.2 (Career Development): Framework becomes demotivating if no one advances</li><li>M5.2d (Retention): Attrition if expectations unmet</li><li>M5.1/M5.3: Talent loss disrupts AI enablement and automation work</li><li>Organizational capability building fails if people leave</li><li>Loss of institutional knowledge and momentum across all methods</li></ul> |
