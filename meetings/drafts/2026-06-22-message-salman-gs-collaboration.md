[GS collaboration — transparency]

In our first 1:1 I flagged some challenges on collaborating with Global Support, and I committed to following up with something concrete. Last Friday's workshop presentation gave me that opportunity — here it is.

The GS-PE collaboration has a recurring pattern I want to name directly. In every sync between us, the agenda, the topics, and the drive come from PE. GS's posture in those sessions is passive — always expecting PE to drive the changes around their complaints, rather than bringing proposals for problems they own. The focus tends to be on tracking: logging what they've done, documenting every interaction, building a record. At one point in those syncs, a list of supposed pending items from PE was shared — without any prior alignment on what the actual ask was or why it was ours to own. That holds more broadly: most of the action items from the March workshop are being driven by Engineering, SRE, and PE.

There's also a scope problem that compounds this. GS expectations sometimes extend to PE influencing SRE's operating model directly — things like moving customer communications from SRE to GS. That's an alignment that needs to happen between GS and SRE. PE doesn't have the empowerment to make those changes, and it doesn't make sense for us to be in the middle of it.

A few examples that make the pattern concrete:

I raised these concerns directly with Pedro and João in March. To their credit, the team started shifting — bringing more proposals than before. The OLA proposal is evidence of that: faced with growing MTTR, they came back with a concrete proposal. But it landed on PE to review last week, after three years of "this is pending on PE" or "we need PE to define this." The deeper problem isn't who leads it — it's that GS has been changing their MTTR targets without prior alignment with R&D, in a scenario where they have a significant dependency on R&D to actually hit those targets. That's the same gap as the enablement problem: expectations set unilaterally, dependency on Engineering assumed. Even so, we're already driving the operating model split between service incidents and support cases. R&D doesn't perceive MTTR the same way GS does — and because PE recognizes that language and framing alignment across all stakeholders is the prerequisite for anything else to work, we decided to move forward with that proposal. Not because it was assigned to us, but because without it, every conversation about targets and dependencies stays stuck.

On ODC enablement: GS has consistently stated that lack of ODC knowledge drives high escalation rates to Engineering, which is the primary driver of higher customer-facing MTTR. There's a workshop action specifically to address this — create a mechanism to scale up GS's service transition motion by giving them structural visibility on R&D releases — and it's not assigned to PE. Last time we followed up, the response was "pending on PE" — with no prior alignment on what they expected from us.

You mentioned in the meeting that our capacity is surpassed. Part of what's driving that is PE absorbing work that isn't ours to lead — because if we don't move it, it doesn't move. That's the structural gap behind the symptom.

There's an opportunity here to fix this properly: define once and for all what GS Service Assurance owns and what PE owns in the continuous improvement cycle. That clarity doesn't exist today, and without it the same dynamic will repeat on every cross-functional initiative we run.
